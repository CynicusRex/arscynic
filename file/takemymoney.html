What if I paid for all my free software?

I've always felt guilty by taking for granted the rare breed of virtuous humans that provide free excellent software without relying on advertising. Let's change that and pay, how much would I “lose” anyway?

Requirements for consideration.

  1. No advertising, trackers, etc., whatsoever.
  2. Free. All of the main features must be available for free.
  3. No already rich people/companies.
  4. No association with or reference to crypto“currencies” because these are greed incentivizing pyramid schemes.
  5. That I'd be quite annoyed if said software didn't exist.

Donation amount calculation.

The donation amount is in proportion to my usage and whether software is in constant development, e.g., an operating system (OS)—not to be confused with malware that is Microsoft Windows, also known as “Windoze” [];

“a shitty operating system created by a monopoly. Commonly used by idiots who are too stupid to RTFM. The only feature that Windoze has that Linux doesn't is the BSOD, commonly seen by Windoze users.” —I hate winblows

Before talking money I feel compelled to admit my bias, when I buy a product, I like to own it. However, considering something that's constantly improving, one cannot expect to pay for it once and receive all subsequent non-security related updates. Time has been invested by its developers, and housing/food is not free for most of us. For example, I don't think it's fair to pay for an OS in the nineties, and expect access to the n-th version of said OS thirty years later. So, in this case a monthly subscription makes sense. Conversely, software that doesn't get updates, or doesn't need to get them, or where the updates don't matter that much to me, a one-time donation seems sufficient. E.g., back in the ignorant days where I still used Microsoft products, I was fine using Office 2010 indefinitely. Obviously now I just pirate that nonsense if I absolutely require it.

So, regarding subscriptions, the highest tier gets €3.50—“tree fiddy” []—a month, the middle tier €2.25, and the default tier €1. These respectively add up to a yearly €42, €27, and €12 donation. One-time donations are €5.

Total amount.

Here we have it, a yearly € bill.

The individual donations may seem trivial, but let's say a computer program has 10000 users—0.0000012% of humanity—and they all pay €5, that's a fairly decent sum. Unfortunately only a minority of people pay for free software, not enough to live on. Conversely, the majority of humanity cannot cough up a yearly ±€400 for donations. But here's the kicker, they shouldn't. People shouldn't be reliant on the whims of others. Ideally, creative people who contribute useful things should be able to do so without having to worry about food or housing. You can already hear me coming: universal basic income.

What would a society look like where people who provide excellent products free of charge, while working a full-time job elsewhere, can pursue their side projects full-time instead?

I've quote Buckminster before and I shall again:

“We must do away with the absolutely specious notion that everybody has to earn a living. It is a fact today that one in ten thousand of us can make a technological breakthrough capable of supporting all the rest. The youth of today are absolutely right in recognizing this nonsense of earning a living. We keep inventing jobs because of this false idea that everybody has to be employed at some kind of drudgery because, according to Malthusian-Darwinian theory, he must justify his right to exist. So we have inspectors of inspectors and people making instruments for inspectors to inspect inspectors. The true business of people should be to go back to school and think about whatever it was they were thinking about before somebody came along and told them they had to earn a living.” —Buckminster Fuller (1895–1983) []
